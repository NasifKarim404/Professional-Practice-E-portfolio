Week 1 - Getting Started

Description: CW1 involved teamwork, while CW2 was an individual task requiring full responsibility.

Feelings: I felt comfortable in CW1 but sometimes frustrated by unequal contribution. In CW2, I felt more independent but also more pressure.

Evaluation: Teamwork helped share ideas, but communication issues affected progress. Working alone improved my focus and time management, though it was challenging.

Analysis: My experience showed that I rely on structure and communication. Lack of teamwork coordination caused stress, while independence required better discipline.

Conclusion: I could have communicated more effectively in CW1 and planned my time better in CW2.

Action Plan: In future, I will improve time management, stay organised, and develop stronger communication skills to perform better in both team and individual tasks.

Week 3 - Milestone Review

Description: Engaging with the fundamentals of creative thinking and problem-solving strengthened my understanding of how effective teamwork and structured reasoning shape successful project outcomes.

Feelings: I felt inspired by the lecture’s focus on perception and questioning assumptions. The discussion on feedback systems made me appreciate the value of diverse perspectives during ideation.

Evaluation: Creative thinking helped me generate better ideas, while rational thinking clarified how to evaluate those ideas systematically. The combination of both approaches made problem definition and software proposal design much stronger.

Analysis: The lecture showed that effective teamwork depends on balancing creativity with structured reasoning. I learned that challenging assumptions and using evidence-based analysis are both essential in developing clear, efficient solutions.

Conclusion: I now understand that balanced creativity, critical evaluation, and rational structure are key to defining problems clearly and proposing more effective software solutions.

Action Plan: I will continue to practice seeing problems from different perspectives, ask better questions, and use systematic analysis to improve my future project planning and feedback design.

Week 4 - Deep Dive

Description: This week, I explored user research, transitioning from demographic personas to behavioral archetypes, and studied professional presentation skills. I also examined the importance of defining a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) and using selection matrices to justify technical decisions.

Feelings: I felt inspired by how psychographics reveal user motivations. I also felt more confident recognizing that soft skills, like clear communication and teamwork, are as critical to my career as technical coding ability.

Evaluation: Learning to objectively evaluate ideas through a selection matrix was a major strength. However, I realized the challenge of explaining complex technical options simply within a five-minute team presentation.

Analysis: These sessions showed that effective software design requires empathy to solve core problems, while professional delivery ensures those solutions are understood. Balancing these ensures that the built artefact matches the user ideal point.

Conclusion: I now understand that technical success depends on iterative research and disciplined, collaborative communication.

Action Plan: I will practice my upcoming presentation, focusing on timed handovers and professional delivery, and use user stories to guide my future development tasks.

Week 5 - Collaboration Focus

Description: This week, I focused on the transition from traditional demographic personas to behavioral archetypes in user research and explored the essential elements of professional group presentations within the technology sector.

Feelings: I felt encouraged to realize that technical success is heavily supported by effective communication. I also found the shift toward psychographics—understanding user motivations and pain points—to be a more insightful way to approach software design than just looking at basic data.

Evaluation: Learning to use selection matrices to objectively justify technology choices was a highlight, as it adds a layer of professional reasoning to project decisions. However, I recognized the challenge of balancing technical depth with the one idea per slide rule during presentations.

Analysis: The sessions highlighted that software design requires deep empathy to solve core problems, while professional delivery ensures those solutions are understood by stakeholders. Integrating these skills is vital for moving from a developer mindset to an industry-ready professional.

Conclusion: I now understand that a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) must be rooted in rigorous user research and that its value is only fully realized when communicated through disciplined, collaborative teamwork.

Action Plan: I will update my e-portfolio with these insights and prepare for my next presentation by practicing timed handovers and focusing on non-verbal cues like eye contact and professional posture.

Week 6 - Technical Challenges

Description: This week, I studied time management strategies, including the Zeigarnik effect, circadian rhythms, and the "Hell Yeah or No" principle. I also reviewed the strict professional requirements for our upcoming five-minute group presentations.

Feelings: I felt relieved to learn that procrastination often stems from open mental loops, but I felt some pressure regarding the presentation's hard five-minute stop and professional dress code.

Evaluation: Identifying my peak energy times through circadian rhythm analysis was highly beneficial. However, I realized I currently struggle with time blocking, often underestimating how long technical tasks take.

Analysis: Managing time is not just about tools; it is a psychological shift toward owning one's schedule. Effective presentation delivery and academic success both rely on closing mental loops and prioritizing high-value tasks over busywork.

Conclusion: I now understand that professional discipline—from punctuality to speech rehearsal—is essential for industry readiness.

Action Plan: I will implement time blocking for my project work and coordinate a final rehearsal to ensure our presentation handovers are seamless and stay within the five-minute limit.

Week 7 - Project Integration

Description: This week focused on academic essay writing and reflecting on the CW1 group presentation. I learned about graduate attributes, the importance of academic integrity, and how to structure a critical assessment rather than a purely descriptive one.

Feelings: I felt a sense of accomplishment after finishing the presentation, but also identified areas for growth. I felt enlightened by the instruction words (e.g., analyze vs. evaluate), which clarified how to approach academic tasks more professionally.

Evaluation: Identifying common presentation mistakes, such as text-heavy slides and weak connections between design and prototypes, was very helpful. However, I realized I need to improve my formal academic writing and referencing to meet university standards.

Analysis: Academic writing is not just about conveying facts; it's about critical thinking and evidence-based arguments. Aligning my project work with professional attributes ensures I am developing the skills employers actually value.

Conclusion: I now understand that success requires a balance between technical prototyping and the ability to critically document and justify my work.

Action Plan: I will use the Essay Instruction Words guide to structure my next report and continue updating my e-portfolio with evidence of my research and feedback.

Week 8 - Review and Feedback

Description: This week focused on advanced academic writing techniques, critical literature reviews, and the structured maintenance of a personal e-portfolio to track professional development (PDP) skills.

Feelings: I felt more organized after learning the specific checklist for essay drafting, though slightly overwhelmed by the rigor required for a critical review compared to a standard summary.

Evaluation: A major strength was learning how to use the Cottrell method for paragraph structure and evidence-based arguments. However, I realized that I need to be more disciplined in my proofreading to ensure a formal, objective tone.

Analysis: I've learned that a successful academic essay requires a strategic plan—starting with a clear outline and using referencing to avoid plagiarism. The e-portfolio acts as a vital bridge between these academic tasks and my future career goals.

Conclusion: Academic success is built on structured planning, critical evaluation of sources, and constant self-reflection.

Action Plan: I will follow the 10-step essay checklist for my upcoming assignments and update my e-portfolio with a dedicated PDP skills section as outlined in the lecture.

Week 9 - Final Preparations

Description: This week focused on the formal mechanics of reflective writing and the application of Gibbs' Reflective Cycle. I explored how to move beyond descriptive writing to achieve critical engagement, ensuring my academic work meets university standards for clarity and objectivity.

Feelings: I felt a shift in my perspective on self-assessment; initially, I viewed reflection as a simple summary, but I now feel more challenged to honestly critique my own biases and decision-making processes.

Evaluation: A major strength was learning the specific stages of the Gibbs cycle, which provides a clear roadmap for analysis. However, I realized that maintaining a formal, objective tone while writing about personal experiences is a skill I still need to refine.

Analysis: Reflection is a bridge between academic theory and professional practice. By systematically challenging my own approach, I can convert raw experiences into actionable intelligence, which is essential for continuous improvement in software development and team collaboration.

Conclusion: I now understand that true reflection requires a balance of honest self-critique and evidence-based planning to ensure future project success.

Action Plan: I will apply the full six-stage Gibbs cycle to my upcoming e-portfolio entries and use the "Academic Instruction Words" guide to ensure my writing remains analytical rather than just descriptive.
